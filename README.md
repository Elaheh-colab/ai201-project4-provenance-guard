# Provenance Guard 🛡️

Provenance Guard is a backend detection system designed for creative sharing platforms. It classifies submitted text to determine whether it was authored by a human or generated by artificial intelligence, surfaces a plain-language transparency label to users, and provides a fully functioning appeals workflow for creators to contest misclassifications.

## Architecture Overview
The system follows a synchronous, multi-signal pipeline:
1. **Input:** A client submits text and a creator ID to the `POST /submit` endpoint.
2. **Gateway:** The request passes through an in-memory Flask-Limiter to prevent spam.
3. **Parallel Analysis:** The text is simultaneously evaluated by two distinct signals: a semantic LLM evaluator and a structural stylometric algorithm.
4. **Scoring Engine:** The raw scores from both signals are mathematically weighted (70/30) into a single confidence score.
5. **Threshold Mapping:** The final score is evaluated against calibrated decision boundaries to determine the correct transparency label (AI, Human, or Uncertain).
6. **Persistence:** The result, alongside the granular signal scores, is saved to an immutable JSON audit log.
7. **Output:** The system returns the final label and confidence score to the client.

---

## Detection Signals
The system relies on two distinct heuristics to evaluate text:

### 1. Semantic Analysis (Groq API - `llama-3.1-8b-instant`)
* **What it measures:** The semantic predictability and structural perfection of the text.
* **Why it was chosen:** Modern LLMs are highly effective at detecting the statistical watermarks of other LLMs, recognizing the sterile, highly probable word choices that characterize AI generation.
* **What it misses:** It struggles heavily with extreme internet slang, all-lowercase text, or run-on sentences. It frequently flags highly informal human text as an "anomaly" and generates false positives for AI.

### 2. Stylometrics (Sentence Length Variance)
* **What it measures:** The mathematical standard deviation of word counts across sentences.
* **Why it was chosen:** Human writers naturally use "bursty" text—mixing short, punchy fragments with long, rambling thoughts. AI models default to highly uniform sentence structures. 
* **What it misses:** It completely fails on very short text snippets (1-3 sentences) because there is not enough data to establish a meaningful variance pattern.

---

## Confidence Scoring & Calibration
Rather than treating both signals equally, the system uses a **70/30 weighted formula**:
`Final Score = (LLM Score * 0.7) + (Stylometric Score * 0.3)`

Because stylometrics are unreliable on short text, the semantic LLM is given the majority authority. During validation, empirical testing showed that the 70/30 split successfully balanced the signals but created a compressed distribution (scores clustered near 0.50). To ensure the scoring produced meaningful variation, the decision boundaries were calibrated to fit the real-world output:

**Example 1: High-Confidence AI Submission**
* *Input:* "Artificial intelligence represents a transformative paradigm shift in modern society. It is important to note that while the benefits of AI are numerous, it is equally essential to consider the ethical implications."
* *Result:* **Score: 0.73** -> `likely_ai`

**Example 2: High-Confidence Human Submission**
* *Input:* "Ive been thinking a lot about remote work lately. There are genuine tradeoffs — flexibility and no commute on one side, isolation and blurred work-life boundaries on the other."
* *Result:* **Score: 0.37** -> `likely_human`

## Transparency Labels
The system surfaces one of three plain-language labels to the user based on the calibrated confidence thresholds.

| Classification | Score Threshold | Verbatim Label Text |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **High-Confidence Human** | `<= 0.40` | "High-Confidence Human: Our analysis indicates a strong likelihood that this content was naturally authored by a human." |
| **Uncertain Origin** | `0.41 - 0.64` | "Uncertain Origin: Our system detected mixed signals. This content may be heavily edited human work, or AI-generated text modified to appear human." |
| **High-Confidence AI** | `>= 0.65` | "High-Confidence AI: Our analysis strongly indicates this content was generated by artificial intelligence." |

## Known Limitations
The system struggles to confidently classify **informal, slang-heavy internet reviews** (e.g., lowercase rants with poor grammar). Because the LLM is trained on formal text, it flags the messy grammar as anomalous and outputs a high AI probability. Meanwhile, the stylometric signal recognizes the messy variance and outputs a low AI probability. The conflicting signals cancel each other out, consistently dropping these human-written edge cases into the "Uncertain Origin" bucket.

## Rate Limiting Configuration
* **Chosen Limits:** `5 per minute; 50 per day`
* **Reasoning:** A human creator actively editing and re-submitting a draft might hit the endpoint a few times in quick succession, but they will not organically submit more than 5 distinct revisions within 60 seconds. This limit provides a generous buffer for normal use while immediately halting automated scripts attempting to exhaust API credits.

## Spec Reflection
* **How the Spec Helped:** The spec's requirement to design an explicit "edge case / uncertain" bucket was crucial. It provided a safe fallback for the conflicting signal scenarios mentioned in the Limitations section, preventing the system from making false accusations.
* **How the Implementation Diverged:** The initial spec called for strict thresholds (>= 0.70 for AI, <= 0.35 for Human). During testing, the system diverged by widening these boundaries to 0.65 and 0.40. This was necessary because the dual-signal architecture mathematically compresses scores toward the middle, meaning the system rarely outputs extreme values on short text snippets.

## AI Usage Log
**Prompt Engineering the LLM Signal:** I consulted an AI assistant to help refine the system prompt for the Groq API, specifically aiming to instruct the model to recognize internet slang and lack of capitalization as human traits. However, we ran into the "Negative Constraint Paradox." By explicitly defining those rules, the LLM overthought the prompt and ended up scoring casual human slang highly as AI (0.74). Seeing this, I chose to override the AI's suggestion, reverted to the standard prompt, and documented this behavior as a known limitation of the system.

**Calibrating the Scoring Math:** I worked with an AI assistant to analyze the score distribution from my initial testing phase, noting that the outputs were clustering heavily in the middle "Uncertain" tier rather than pushing toward the definitive labels. Through this consultation, the AI suggested tweaking the mathematical calibration to a 70/30 signal split and slightly widening the `if/elif` threshold logic to better fit the empirical data. I integrated these adjustments, which successfully spread the distribution, pushing the AI-generated texts above the 0.65 threshold and the human texts below 0.40.

## API Documentation & Audit Log

### 1. `POST /submit`
Accepts text for analysis and returns a classification label.
* **Payload:** `{"text": "string", "creator_id": "string"}`
* **Returns:** `201 Created` with content ID, confidence score, and transparency label.

### 2. `POST /appeal`
Allows creators to contest a classification label.
* **Payload:** `{"content_id": "uuid", "creator_reasoning": "string"}`
* **Returns:** `200 OK` and updates the internal audit log status to `under_review`.
